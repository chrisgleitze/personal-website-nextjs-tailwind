Here are three lessons I learned from coding that are not specific to programming languages. The first two lessons are about removing typing and navigation as productivity bottlenecks. This is what I would've loved to hear when I started out coding. Those two will make you significantly faster. The third one is advice on where to get inspiration from. These lessons transfer to other knowledge work as well, as I will show at the end.

## Get good at touch typing

Practice daily for at least 10 to 20 minutes on a website like Monkeytype. Practice until you easily write over 100 words per minute. Even with AI assistance, you will still type a lot if you want to do any amount of knowledge work — whether it's writing code, prompts, documentation, prose, research papers, articles on LinkedIn, e-mails, etc. If you're working at a computer, fast typing removes one of the most basic friction points. Once you stop looking at the keyboard, you free up attention for the actual problem you're solving.

## Know how to navigate quickly in your terminal and code editor

Quick navigation is key to get to your destination fast. It's important to not waste mental energy on long manual searches for files in big software repositories. You need to save your mental capacity for the challenging tasks. The first step is to add the most popular fuzzy finder to your system, appropriately called [fzf](https://github.com/junegunn/fzf). Then you can use Ctrl-r and Ctrl-t to fuzzy search your command history and for files respectively. You can also set up a custom keybinding for directory search (I use Alt-s).

The second is to add aliases to your bash or zsh config, at least for paths that you frequently use, like your editor config (Neovim in my case) and the directory that contains your coding projects:

```
alias cdn='cd $HOME/.config/nvim'
alias cdp='cd $HOME/projects'
```

You could navigate there with fzf as well, but typing cdn is still quicker. Though I admit, this is a small win compared to going from manual navigation to fzf in the first place.

The third step is to use a fuzzy finder in your code editor, so you can search for files and specific code much more quickly. For Vim and Neovim there's really good fzf integrations. In my opinion the best option for Vim is [fzf.vim](https://github.com/junegunn/fzf.vim) by the creator of the fzf tool mentioned above. For Neovim I can recommend [fzf-lua](https://github.com/ibhagwan/fzf-lua) which is super fast and you can configure all kinds of searches tailored to your needs.

## Get inspiration from the right people

When I started out learning Vim and Neovim I didn't know where to look, especially when it came to the configuration. Now looking back I would recommend going through the configuration files of the maintainers and top contributors of Vim and Neovim. By going through their configurations you learn how the professionals do it.

For other technologies it is similar: Look for popular projects on GitHub and see who the top contributors are. They usually have repos you can learn a lot from. If you're learning a web framework, read the source of a well-regarded open-source app built with it. If you're learning a language, study the standard library. It's usually written by the best programmers in that ecosystem.

Here's one concrete example of what you find when you dig through expert configurations. This handy command from [my Neovim configuration](https://github.com/chrisgleitze/init.lua) edits all occurrences of a word in a file, if I press the leader key and then the s-key twice. I borrow it from [the Neovim config](https://github.com/ThePrimeagen/init.lua/blob/72a1a7ecd96bb44b8958b26641da674d569b87bc/lua/theprimeagen/remap.lua#L49) of a popular YouTube programmer.

```
-- change all occurrences of the word the cursor is on in a file
map('n', '<leader>ss', [[:%s/\<<C-r><C-w>\>/<C-r><C-w>/gI<Left><Left><Left>]])
```

## How does this translate to knowledge work?

Knowledge work is work that mainly consists of thinking minus physical labor. Of course a bricklayer is thinking as well but in order to do physical labor. A knowledge worker like a Software Engineer or a Professor of Philosophy is thinking but not in order to do heavy physical labor. I use many of the tools and workflows from programming in my work on my doctoral thesis in Philosophy, and I've found that the three lessons above map directly.

**Reduce friction in your output medium.** Whatever your primary medium of output is, make the mechanics effortless. When I'm working on my PhD thesis in Ethics of AI, I can type out my ideas quickly because I can type fast on my keyboard. For a mathematician it might be fluency with LaTeX notation. For someone in a more verbal field it could be dictation. The principle is the same: Don't let the mechanics of producing output be the bottleneck.

**Organize your information retrieval.** The navigation lesson is about making lookup cheap, so you can save your mental energy for the hard tasks. In programming that means fuzzy finders and aliases. In academic work it means a good reference manager like Zotero, a note-linking system like Obsidian or a Zettelkasten, and consistent file naming conventions. I use fzf to search through my research notes and PDF annotations the same way I use it to navigate code. The tool is the same, the principle is the same.

**Study the workflows of the best in your field.** The inspiration lesson translates very well. In knowledge work more broadly, study not just what the best thinkers in your field argue but how they work — how they structure their reading, take notes, and build arguments. Read their methodological reflections, watch their lectures, look at their bibliographies. Below I list some resources I recommend.

Remove the bottlenecks of your workflow and get inspired by the best, so there's as few restrictions for you to think. Then read and write a lot and discuss your ideas with other people.

## Recommended sources of inspiration

### Philosophy

It's necessary to get the basics right. So, let's start there. There are many PDFs by academic institutions about writing philosophical papers flying around the internet. Jim Pryor's Guidelines on Writing a Philosophy Paper is one of the most popular texts online about philosophical writing because it simply is really good. "A philosophy paper consists of the reasoned defense of some claim", he begins. The rest is every bit as direct as you'd expect after that beginning. Holm Tetens wrote an introductory book on philosophical reasoning which is also a good starting point.
- Pryor, Jim: Guidelines on Writing a Philosophy Paper. ([link](https://www.jimpryor.net/teaching/guidelines/writing.html) to the full text)
- Tetens, Holm: _Philosophisches Argumentieren_. Beck. München 2004.

This is the best biography on Immanuel Kant. Though you'll likely only like it if you're interested in Kant's philosophy. It's available both in English and German.

- Kühn, Manfred: _Kant: A Biography._ Cambridge University Press. Cambridge 2001.

### General

No matter in what discipline you're a knowledge worker, you will surely find very good guidelines on getting the basics right in the world wide web. It might be guidelines on writing clean code, writing conventions in Mathematics or how to write a paper for a journal in Political Science.

To get a better idea of clear prose and the process of writing, I liked:

- Strunk, William Jr., and White, E.B.: _The Elements of Style_. Longman. New York 1999.
- Zinsser, William: _On Writing Well_. HarperCollins. New York 2006.
- McPhee, John: _Draft No. 4_. Farrar, Straus & Giroux Inc. New York 2017.

A great source of inspiration are interviews with famous writers. I looove the interviews in The Paris Review. The ones with John McPhee and Haruki Murakami are good. Another good source of inspiration are biographies of intellectuals, artists and people who achieved a lot. Here are some I liked a lot:

- Isaacson, Walter: _Benjamin Franklin: An American Life_. Simon & Schuster. New York 2004.
- Isaacson, Walter: _Leonardo da Vinci_. Simon & Schuster. New York 2017.
- Morris, Edmund: _The Rise of Theodore Roosevelt_. Coward, McCann & Geoghegan. New York 1979.
